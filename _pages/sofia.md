<p><strong>The SOFIA project aims to uncover the key physical processes that move heat between the atmosphere, the ocean surface, and the deep ocean in the stormy Southern Ocean.</strong> This is motivated by two main questions: how do powerful storms set the exchange of heat between air and sea? and how do swirling ocean fronts, eddies, and narrow filaments transport that heat into the ocean interior?</p>

<p>What motivates these questions is almost all (93%) of the excess heat caused human-made greenhouse gases ends up in the ocean. SOFIA aims to uncover what regulates this heat uptake so we can understand and predict its future changes.</p>

<p>We focus on the Southen Ocean due to its outsized role in this regard. It acts like a vast heat sponge, taking up as much as 75–83% of the total heat stored by the global ocean. Yet, despite its importance, we still struggle to agree on exactly how and why this region is so effective at absorbing heat. Climate models can disagree by as much as 40%, largely because they don’t capture the fine-scale winds, eddies, and turbulent motions that dominate these waters.</p>

<p>To tackle these challenges, SOFIA will combine <strong>cutting-edge coupled ocean-atmosphere simulations</strong> with new in-situ observations collected by <strong>autonomous robotic platforms</strong> in one of the most remote parts of the planet. I also use data from ESA’s <strong>Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) satellite</strong> to map vertical ocean motion across the entire Southern Ocean.</p>

<p>Together, these efforts strive to build a clearer and more predictive understanding of how the ocean regulates Earth’s climate — knowledge that’s essential for improving future climate projections.</p>
